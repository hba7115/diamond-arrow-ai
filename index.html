What is New World Screwworm?

New World Screwworm (NWS) flies lay eggs in open wounds or body openings of live, warm-blooded animals. The larvae (maggots) burrow into living flesh, causing serious injury and often death if untreated.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Threat: NWS can infest cattle, horses, sheep, goats, pigs, pets, wildlife, and in rare cases, humans. This is a threat to our ranching community, food supply, and national security.

Current Situation

NWS has spread northward through Central America and Mexico. Active cases are confirmed in Tamaulipas, Mexico - directly across the Texas border. The parasite was eradicated from the U.S. in 1966, but is now threatening to return.

Texas Response

A joint Texas NWS Response Team has been established by the Texas Animal Health Commission (TAHC) and Texas Parks and Wildlife Department (TPWD). A $750 million Sterile Fly Production Facility is under construction in Edinburg, Texas.

How to Identify NWS

ü™∞ Adult NWS Fly Appearance:

  • Size: About the size of a common housefly or slightly larger
  • Eyes: Orange/reddish-orange
  • Body: Metallic blue or green
  • Back: Three dark stripes along their back

Signs of Infestation in Animals:

  • Depression, isolation from herd
  • Head shaking, irritation
  • Biting or licking at wounds
  • Foul odor from wounds
  • Small white eggs or larvae around wounds
  • Drainage or enlargement around nose, ears, umbilicus, or genitalia
Important: Only NWS feed on LIVING tissue. Other fly larvae feed on dead tissue. If you see maggots in a wound on a live animal, report it immediately.

Prevention & Protection

  • Inspect animals regularly - especially after calving, branding, or any procedure that creates wounds
  • Keep wounds clean and covered - work with your veterinarian on preventative treatments
  • Monitor body openings - nose, ears, umbilicus, genitalia
  • When traveling to/from affected areas - inspect pets and vehicles for NWS flies and larvae
  • Report ALL suspicious cases - early detection is critical
